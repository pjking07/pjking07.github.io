Teen authors Alex and Brett Harris recently released their first book, Do Hard Things, with the tag-line, "A teenage rebellion against low expectations." The Harris twins are nineteen and are currently freshmen in college. They have administered the website and blog "The Rebelution" since 2005.

This book is engaging from the start. The opening paragraph reads:

Most people don't expect you to understand what we're going to tell you in this book. And even if you understand, they don't expect you to care. And even if you care, they don't expect you to do anything about it. And even if you do something about it, they don't expect it to last.

Well, we do.

The authors begin their book by reviewing the history of adolescence and emphasizing the newness of our cultural concept of the "teenager". The book touches on the history of some people who did big (hard) things during their teen years, including George Washington and Clara Barton. The authors then call for the reader to redefine their view of adolescence and see it not as a time of idleness before adulthood, but rather as a launching pad for adulthood.

This book was written as an encouragement for teenagers to do hard things. The Harris brothers message is directed towards teenagers, but readers of all ages would benefit from the authors' exhortations. The authors list five things that they particularly have in mind when mention hard things:

  1. Things that are outside your comfort zone
  2. Things that go beyond what is expected or required
  3. Things that are too big to accomplish alone
  4. Things that don't earn an immediate payoff
  5. Things that challenge the cultural norm

Throughout the book, the authors exemplify this list with many stories of the hard things done by youth today. The examples they include are definitely encouraging.

The chapter that was most convicting for me was "Raising the Bar". This portion of the book encourages youth to battle complacency. According to the authors, it takes little effort for some youth to be considered "above average". They may be naturally inclined to do better than most (youth) in academics, sports, or music. An external cause of complacency, say the authors, is that youth are not held to a high enough standard. Those who naturally perform above this low standard have little reason to take on a challenge. An internal cause of complacency is that the teenagers who easily excel in some areas refuse to challenge themselves in other fields which are more difficult for them. This refusal can stem from a fear of failure (or of mediocrity). The authors' encouragement is for youth to do those things in which they excel, but also to challenge themselves by doing hard things.

Another important concept is found in the chapter "Small Hard Things". Most of the book is about doing big hard things. This chapter encourages the reader to also do small hard things which are hard because:

  • They don't usually go away after you do them [such as cleaning]
  • They don't seem very important
  • They don't seem to make any difference
  • They don't seem very glamorous
  • No one is watching

This chapter provides great encouragement for the reader to do the small things well. As Luke 16:10 (NIV) reads, “Whoever can be trusted with very little can also be trusted with much, and whoever is dishonest with very little will also be dishonest with much.”

Although this book is a great encouragement for youth of all ages to do hard things, sometimes the reading feels like a standard self-help book. I counted six different action plans throughout the book. This specific critique is not for everyone; I am simply not a big fan of generic action plans.

Do Hard Things is a courageous and encouraging book from Alex and Brett Harris; I know these men will be influential for years to come. I strongly recommend this book to all ages; it will not only encourage younger (and older) readers to do harder things, but it will also encourage older readers to raise their expectations of youth.
